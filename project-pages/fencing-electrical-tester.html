Fencing Testbox
Project Details / Background
This is my main passion project to make an inexpensive electrical tester for fencing equipment, namely blades and body cords. I initially started this project because I wanted something to make my own work easier at the UCSB Fencing Club, where I was the armorer and repaired most things, but I kept going to learn new technologies for myself and maybe commercialize this as a product. Existing professional testers are very expensive for the purpose that they serve for student athletes, while the next best thing is a multimeter which is a hassle to use.
In fencing, there are three different types of blades: Foil, Epee, and Sabre. For these blades, there are two different types of body cords: Epee cords, and Foil/Sabre cords. Each kind has three wires, but one side of the wires has a different connection. Pictured below are the two kinds:
The two main faults a body cord can have is a faulty connection at a prong, or a break somewhere along the wire. Testing this is easy by looking for electrical continuity in the wire. With a multimeter, this can be done by looking at the resistance of the wire: very low resistance indicates a good connection, and very high resistance or no connection indicates a fault. However, this is time consuming and finicky, as a user has to manually hold the leads of a multimeter to the prongs of a wire. Testing blades works in a similar way, but will not be discussed because it is different for each weapon.
What my design accomplishes is streamlining the testing of all three wires in a body cord, so it can be done at the same time with one device. Additionally, my design integrates blade testing functionality so that a user wouldn't have to purchase a separate product for that purpose. I started out by making a proof of concept in a cardboard box using spare parts from fencing sockets. This was crude, but was already making my work much easier.
Instead of using a multimeter, an LED indicated an electrical connection. Though this isn't precise, it gets the job done.
This was my first 3D printed prototype. I experimented with a design that had sockets on three surfaces of the device which made it very compact, but settled on using just the top surface to facilitate moving over to using a PCB instead of hand soldering every component.
This is the inside of the first 3D printed prototype. One of the issues with making a compact 3D circuit was that assembling it was annoying.
For my second prototype, I simplified the circuit and moved all the electrical connections to the top surface of the device. Shown above is how an epee body cord would be tested. Because all three LED's are illuminated, that indicates a good body cord. The two unlit LED's and empty sockets at the bottom are for blade testing.
This was the inside of the second prototype. Though this was still hand soldered, assembly was much easier than before. I still needed to resolve battery placement though.
For the third prototype, I modified the housing by adding testing functionality for foil body cords.
On the inside of the housing of the third prototype, I added a dedicated place for the battery.
For the fourth and final prototype, I learned how to design and order a custom PCB using KiCad and PCB Way. I used the knowledge I gained from this phase of the project in future projects as well, such as the Automatic Bicycle Shifter.
This is the inside of the assembled final tester. Putting this together was a breeze compared to the previous iterations of this product, but it still had some minor fitment issues.
This is the final assembled tester. I can edit this version forever trying to come up with a perfect design, but I think that this is a good stopping point with its current functionality. For a more advanced tester that can compete with professional equipment, I am looking into incorporating a microprocessor for displaying resistance values of wires being tested, as well as switching between different modes for different testing scenarios.
